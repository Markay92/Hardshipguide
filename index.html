Why Interest Gets Overwhelming

With high interest rates, most of your payment can go to interest, not your actual debt. This makes it feel impossible to get ahead.

Warning Signs:

  • Your balance never seems to drop.
  • You're using one card to pay another.
  • You're avoiding your statements.

Step 1 of 8

Know What Qualifies as a Hardship

Banks need a clear reason: job loss, reduced hours, medical emergencies, divorce, or a significant increase in living costs.

Step 2 of 8

Use the Right Channel to Contact Them

Use your bank's phone line or secure online chat. Ask for the "financial hardship" department. Act before you fall far behind.

Step 3 of 8

Clearly Explain Your Situation

Show them you want to cooperate. Explain what changed, your current income status, and that you want to find a solution together.

Step 4 of 8

Ask What Programs Are Available

Inquire about specific options like a lower APR, a fixed monthly payment, waived fees, or a plan that keeps your account reported as "current."

Step 5 of 8

Understand All the Terms

Before agreeing, ask: How will this affect my credit? Can I still use my card? Is the interest rate fixed? How long does the plan last?

Step 6 of 8

Be Prepared to Make a Payment

Most issuers require the first payment immediately to enroll you in the program. Be ready for this step.

Step 7 of 8

Stick to the Plan

Always pay on time. Set up automatic payments to protect your progress. Missing payments can get you removed from the program.

Step 8 of 8

Know the Impact on Your Credit

A hardship plan is visible to lenders but doesn't usually hurt your score if you pay on time. It can, however, affect future applications for new credit.

Real-Life Example: Amex Chat

I'm interested in the financial relief program.

Amex Rep: To help, can you share the reason for your financial situation?

A decline in income, and expenses have gone up a lot. I'm struggling.

Amex Rep: You are eligible for our Long-Term Program: 9.99% APR, fixed monthly payments, no late fees, and your account is reported as current.

What If Your Bank Won't Help?

If you're denied, ask about nonprofit credit counseling agencies. They partner with banks to create Debt Management Plans (DMPs).

Be Proactive, Not Reactive

The worst thing you can do is wait. Banks are far more likely to help before you're in deep default. Honesty and a willingness to cooperate are your greatest assets.
